How did strict gender roles and heavy emphasis on religion in early modern Europe (1450 - 1750) influence views on witchcraft?

Malleus Maleficarum

By: Heinrich Kramer + Jacob Sprenger in Speyer, Germany - 1486

Several surviving copies, one housed in the Library of Congress

In this hectic engraving, Bruegel draws an assortment of grotesque imps running rampant, witches flying on animals, and even the Devil himself supervising the mutilation of a man. In the process, Bruegel, living through the peak of European witch hunts, also portrays the common belief of the time that witches received supernatural powers in exchange for becoming servants of the Devil. He depicts them as using these powers to destroy Church steeples and kill livestock. This representation of witches portrays them as a real and present enemy of Christendom, therefore explaining why people in the early modern period feared them so much, not just because of their maleficia, or harmful sorcery, but also because they renounced Christianity whose values served as the basis of European society.

Examination of Martha Carrier - Salem, Massachusetts - 1692

Taking place during the infamous Salem Witch Trials, Martha Carrier was accused of dealing in witchcraft by the "afflicted" girls, neighbors, and even her own children (who were under torture), and thus was hanged as a witch on August 19, 1692. She was an easy target since she had an independent spirit and was unsubmissive. She had illegitimate children with someone from a lower class and received an inheritance of a significant amount of money and property, so you can probably see why she was targeted. She was one of the first victims of the mass hysteria.

Read the transcript at the University of Virginia's Salem Witch Trials Document Collection.

Wallington, a puritan artisan in 17th century England, describes how a coven of witches were discovered by witch-hunter, Matthew Hopkins.
